Gideon sighs and settles into his chair. His blue puffer jacket hangs off the back, limp and lifeless. Gideon wraps his hand around his paper coffee cup and takes a sip.

Where did it all go so wrong? At this thought he removes his black ribbed beanie to rest his head against his palms. Lisa. How many more chances would she give him?

Everything had been so easy growing up. Living here in Saratoga Springs, mom and dad had taken care of everything. Nice houses, nice vacations, and no stress. Never did Gideon still expect to be in his hometown fifty years later.

Gideon stares blankly at the wall. He takes off his small wireframe glasses, fogs each lens, and wipes them with his sweatshirt.

1 week. That’s how long his boss had given him to pull his act together. Lisa was threatening divorce. All because of the damn drink. All because they didn’t understand.

Bourbon was his way to cool off after work. Life was so stressful; didn’t everyone need something to help them relax? Did he not put food on the table? Did he not pay for the kids absurdly expensive private school?

Yes, he did. He was the provider, and he deserved a way to destress. What was Lisa so mad about anyway? Him disappearing on weekends? Coming home 4 AM Monday morning? He was just having fun.

If everyone could just calm down and leave him alone, everything would be fine. If they could all just let him drink, there wouldn’t be any problems.

At this, Gideon hears Lisa’s tearful voice in his head. “Gideon you need to stop, you’re killing yourself! Please. If not for me, do it for the kids.”

Gideon closes his eyes and leans back in his chair. She just didn’t understand. No one understands. If everyone was reasonable, life wouldn’t be so damn difficult.

Gideon checks his watch. 5:00 PM. About time to pick up his quart of bourbon for the night. He picks up his beanie and smiles to himself.

What could Lisa really do? Divorce him? Take the kids? No. She wouldn’t. She loved him, didn’t she? And anyway, he’d just have one drink tonight. This quart would easily last him the week.

He just needed something to decompress. It had been a rough day at work. Just one.

With that, Gideon stands up, tosses out his quarter-full cup of coffee, and walks out.

Post Malone buzzes in the background, faintly audible over the murmur of voices. The air is cool, it feels as if there's a slight draft coming from my right.

There are different flavors of voice all around. To my right excited exaltation ensues, in front of me serious tones deep in discourse, behind me lively exclamations coupled with table thumping.

The song changes, it's still Post Malone, just a sadder melody. TikTok turns on in front of me. I recognize the click of the snare drums in the short clips. A howl breaks out behind me, “Let's gooooooo”, followed by more table pounding.              

A laugh ripples through the air, deep and hearty. There's a faint scent of tomato sauce mixed with grease; everything else passes unnoticed by my stuffy nose.          

The collective hum has quieted to a whisper. The air feels cooler than before, like someone has opened a door or window.

I take a sip of my drink. Cold, the ice knock against my teeth. Sweet, I can taste the lemonade and cranberry swirling together as they roll through my mouth. I'm left with a slightly tart aftertaste and a strong desire to take another sip.                          

This time I get a small piece of ice. I swirl it around with my tongue and and then pulverize it with a crunch.

A fridge closes in the distance, and a pot clangs in a sink.

The music has turned off now, though I think I hear the faint “zzzzz” of a radio somewhere in the distance.

The room moves like an ocean. Voices, like waves, swell to a peak and then crash into silence on the shore. They draw back into the sea with a collective sigh.          

Post Malone turns back on. I recognize the song, but don't know the name. “We're running out of reasons, but we can't let go.” Hollywood is Bleeding. I guess I do know the name.

Three sharp raps on a table behind me grab my attention. I can't make out the words, but the tone of the conversation is jovial. Someone squeals out a laugh.        

And then it becomes clear: the topic is girls. The mood changes, and an argument starts about the timeline of meeting two female friends. “I literally spoke to her about the day… why are you trying to tell me?!”

A voice to my right cuts in, “We're closing, please bring your dishes to the front.” I nod, still enraptured in the moment. As I move to leave, Senorita by Sean Mendes starts to play. A fitting way to close out the night.
